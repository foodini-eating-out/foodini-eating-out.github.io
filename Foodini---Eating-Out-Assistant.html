MILESTONE 1

PROBLEM

Going to a restaurant is a willing decision, although sometimes we’re in a hurry and we just want to stop by for a quick refuel. And in the same way, ordering takeout can waste precious minutes and attention time, distracting people from work and interrupting their flow. What’s more, it can be even more bothersome when the end result is unsatisfactory and we notice that what we ordered doesn’t meet our expectations, or it doesn’t excite us in ways food usually should. Thus, we can often feel overburdened by the sheer number of available options, shivering at the fear of mispicking or pondering too long on the missed opportunity cost. Ordering food should not be this sweat-inducing and we should feel like always having a concierge recommending the best to our tastes, be it at home, or on the go.

SOLUTION

Foodini aims to solve just that. Whether it’s by giving you personal recommendations that are ‘true and tested’ and sure to fit your personal food profile and taste, or by taking you along the untrodden path into culinary exploration to spice up your culinary experiences, the algorithms behind Foodini bring the same ‘I’m feeling lucky’ charm and vibe that staple products such as Google or Spotify have in their arsenal to the food industry.

This has a two-fold benefit. While the benefit for the consumer is clear, there is also an incentive for businesses to take part, in particular a better overview and customization of their menu, topped on more efficient allocation of resources and waste management.

TARGETED CUSTOMER SEGMENT

The foodservice industry serves a vast audience, but we can cherry pick two segments in particular whose needs are met by Foodini:

  • The ‘fire-and-forget’, busy individuals, who don’t have the time in their lives to be bothered with picking food, so they would rather have someone else (or, in this case, something else) pick for them the best option.
  • The gourmet explorers, those who are curious to try out new things and keep their meals exciting and new, but who may lack the time or desire to spend hours reading reviews, blog posts, or stay up to date with the latest places in town.

COMPETITION


Such a food curation platform does not exists yet, as we will be pioneers of a new category, that of personal eating out assistants. However, our scope does overlap with three other categories in particular:

  • Food delivery services, such as Glovo, as they also provide exploration options, but no ‘fire-and-forget’ quick way out and no personalised recommendations based on preferences and patterns.
  • Local exploration apps, such as Foursquare, as they can be useful to those on the go and in a hurry and looking for the nearest place, or to those wanting to try out the latest in town. The platforms, compared to Foodini, do not provide the same curated experience, acting more as a static database than a personal concierge service.
  • Nutrition & meal planner apps, such as Yazio, as they do provide meal suggestions based on personal preferences and nutritional requirements. However, these do not take into account food venues, but rather they suggest prep-at-home meals and try-it-on-your-own recipes

KEY METRICS

The number one core metric is the number of orders placed + meals had out in the city. The more meals people use Foodini for, the higher our engagement and the better our recommendation algorithms.

Other core metrics essential to the survival of the platform are:

  • The number of food venues joining the platform on the supply side
  • The number of customers who can be served based on location (e.g. the number of cities we operate in)

COST STRUCTURE

One-time expenses:
  - MVP + initial deployment
 - Launch campaigns (marketing, first-time discounts, etc.)
 - Potential payment to big players to join the platform (on the supply side)

Ongoing expenses:
 - Development costs
 - Service & support
 - Marketing costs (advertising)
 - Logistical costs
 - Deals & rewards

REVENUE STREAMS


  • Percentage-based revenue on ordered food, taxing the venues
  • Percentage-based revenues on dine-in local recommendations (users have the incentive to integrate Foodini as the third-party as their consumption is tied to a point-based system, which in turn hands our periodic rewards), taxing the venues
  • Pro subscription plans, taxing the consumers

IDENTIFYING THE PROBLEM

We are regular users of food delivery apps. However, we noticed that sometimes we’re spending too much time deciding what to get, or we are hit with decision fatigue. This is how we identified that there’s room for a solution simplifying the process, as no current (local) apps offer such an easy way out for those who don’t know what to order. Upon asking more of our friends, we encountered the same issue on their end, too - they sometimes just don’t know what to get and feel it would save them time if food delivery apps were smarter, in a manner similar to home assistants. 

MILESTONE 2

WHAT WE THOUGHT IS A SOLUTION FOR THE PROBLEM

We immediately thought of putting pattern analysis and AI to good use, as they seem to be the perfect fit towards building a smart recommendation algorithm. The end goal, as we envisioned it, is a platform which knows what you want to eat even when you don’t and it quickly recommends you something, saving you both time and mental effort, while also potentially saving you money. A ‘I’m feeling lucky’ type-of-solution, if you will.

CUSTOMER DISCOVERY PLAN

Considering the nature of our solution (i.e. an improvement over current solutions, not necessarily creating a new category, but rather refining an existing one), we thought of looking into the audiences of current food delivery apps, believing that there’s an unsatisfied audience to ‘steal’ there.

PROCESS DESCRIPTION IN DETAIL

Customer Segments

We identified our customer segments by looking atinternational food delivery services analyses, which indicated some usage patterns and demographics which we should be aiming for. After getting an initial feel of the target demographics, we decided to get such data for ourselves, representative of the local market.

How we reached them

We performed quantitative analysis by deploying a survey, from which we also collected leads for our eventual qualitative analysis, through interviews.


Here is the list of questions and findings:

More than 50% of people eat weekly in the city or order food.

+Previous +Next

36.6% of the people who ​answered the questions consider that they will decide more easily if they would have personalized recommendations

When asked what criteria they rely on when choosing the restaurant, 57.9% of people said they take into account recommendations from friends.

INSIGHTS AFFECTING OUR PRODUCT

The insights lead us towards a better understanding of the needs of the customers, showing us that there’s a market of 40% from the total which would benefit from the solution suggested by us. We also identified the reasons behind the lack of decision making, thus giving us some leads when it comes to designing our algorithms.

ARE WE READY TO MOVE TO THE CUSTOMER VALIDATION STAGE?

Yes, we believe we have narrowed down our audience well enough to dive into the CV stage, for two reasons in particular: we have gathered a set of assumptions about our target audience (their needs and reasoning behind such needs) and we have the data we need to start delving into MVP development, thus requiring that we undertake CV first in order to develop the right product.

USER PERSONA

USER STORIES

What Maria wants:

  • She wants to have recommendations of vegetarian food
  • Due to lack of options for vegetarian food, she wants to receive different dishes
  •  She wants to receive recommendations based on what she liked from her previous order
  • She wants to have the possibility to view only restaurants with vegetarian food by using a custom filter (e.g., categories of food)  

What Andrei wants:

  • He wants to see the ingredients for all dishes
  • He wants to have the possibility to exclude/remove some ingredients from dishes
  •  He wants a filter for prices
  • He wants to see reviews for every dish and to give a review for dishes after he order something   
  • He wants to receive recommendations based on the nutritional values of the dishes
